The word Bheṣajī has been used for medicine in the Atharvaveda. The Atharvaveda has listed not only several plants in general but also some families of the plants such as Asiknī, Kṛṣṇā, Pṛśni, Prastṛṇatī, Stamvinī, Ekaśuṅga, Pratanvatī, Aṃśumatī, Kāṇḍinī, Viśākhā, Vaiśvadevī, Ugrā, Avakā, Ulva, Tīkṣṇaśṛṅgī, as also Puṣpavatī, Prasūmatī, Phalinī, Aphalā etc. The hymn VIII.7 gives a detailed classification of the plants according to their colour, growth, properties, origin etc. 
* First, categorization based on colour like Brabhrū, Śukra, Rohīṇi, Pṛśni, Asiknī, Kṛṣṇā. 

* Second, classification based on form like Prastṛṇatī, Stamvinī, Ekaśuṅga etc. 

* Thirdly, classification based on quality like Jīvalā, Arundhatī, Madhumatī etc. 

* Fourth, classification is based on fruits etc. like Puṣpavatī, Prasūmatī, Phalinī etc. 
There are plants which remove the effects of poison–Viṣadūṣanī and also effects of witchcraft–Kṛtyādūṣaṇī.

## The Charm System
The Ātharvaṇ is primarily a charm system in which the charm was systematically accompanied by an amulet. There are special charms to cure Apachit (AV–VI.25, 57, 83; VII.74, 76), Kāsa (VI. 105, 107), Kilāsa (I.23, 24), Śirṣakti (I.7; IV.12), Jalodara (I.10; VI.22, 24, 96; VII.83), Jāyānya (VII.76.3, 4, 5), Mutrāvarodha (I.3), Śleṣma (I.12, 13; VI.105, 107), Harimā (I.22), Kṣipta (VI.109), Unmāda (VI.111), Pakṣaghāta (VI.80), Kṣetriya (II.8, 10; III.7), Rudhirasrāva (I.17; VI.127; IX.8.1; XII.4.4), Āsrāva (I.2; II.3; VI.44), Tṛṣṇā (II.29), Asthibhaṅga (IV.12), Balāsa (VI.14, 127), Keśavṛddhi (VI.21, 30, 136, 137), Vājīkaraṇa (IV.4; VI.72, 101; VII.95), Grāhi (VI.112, 113), Viṣkandha (II.4; III.9), Takman (I.25; V.4,22; VI.20; VII.116; XIX.39), Sarpaviṣanāśana (V.13; VI.12, 52, 56, 88, 90, 93, 100; X.4), Kṛmināśana (V.23; II.31, 32; VI.50), Vṛśchikaviṣanāśana (VII.56) and Sarvarogabhaiṣajjyaṃ (II.33; III.11; IV.13; V.30; IX.8) and some of these charms mention plants to be used against these diseases.

Thus, the use of Devamuni against Apachit (VII.7); Varaṇa against Yakṣma; Pippalī against Kṣipta; Cīpudru against Balāsa; Nitatnī for Keśavṛddhi; Suparṇāsuri against Harimā; Pṛśniparṇi against miscarriage; Rohiṇī against fracture; Apāmārga against various diseases; Śilācī, Lākṣā for growth of body; Kuṣṭha against Takman; Jaṅgiḍa against Viṣkandha; Viṣāṇakā against Āsrāva. In addition to these, Atharvaveda mentions several plants whose use cannot be specified.

In the Vedas, medicine has been categorized based on its quality, religion and form. The effect of such plants as Rohiṇī can be understood if we consider their etymology.

## Some Herbs in the Atharvaveda
The following are some of the herbs mentioned in the Atharvaveda –

Aghadviṣṭā– It is Dūrvā. Its other names are Devajātā, Śapathayopanī and Sahasrakāṇḍa. It is the destroyer of evil effects arising from pāpanāśana, śāpanāśana and all kinds of the evil eye etc.

Ajaśṛṅgī- This is Meṣaśṛṅgī. It is a Virut plant and has a pungent odour. Its name is ‘Arāṭakī’ i.e. an enemy-destroyer. Again, it is called Ajaśṛṅgī or Tīkṣṇaśṛṅgī as it is a horn-shaped fruit. It kills various diseases and germs.

Atividdha bheṣajī- This is the synonym of Pippalī. Other names for this are kṣiptabheṣajī, pippalī, bātīkṛtabheṣajī. Sāyaṇācārya has given provision for administering this medicine for arthritis, convulsions etc. According to Kauśika Sūtra, it is fed along with some other medicines to increase the talent.

Adṛṣṭadahaṇī–It is mentioned in the Paippalāda Saṃhitā. It is rakṣoghna and kṛmighna. It is beneficial in acne and osteoarthritis.

Aparājitā–This is mentioned in the Paippalāda branch of Atharvaveda. The use of this drug has been mentioned in the Atharva-Pariśiṣṭa as rakṣoghna, kṛtyādūṣaṇa, yaśa, and Barca etc.

Apaskambha–According to Sāyaṇa, Apaskambha means Kramuka tree. The bow is made from it. According to Kauśika Sūtra, it is Sarpaviṣoghna.

Abhiroruda–According to Sāyaṇa, it is ‘Sauvarcala’. It is applied for subjugation. It is called Āsurī medicine. By this, Indra extended his dominion over the gods. It is useful for subduing the husband. In the Paippalāda Saṃhitā, it is called Tejovardhaka and Vaśīkaraṇa.

Abhrighātā–It is mentioned in the Paippalāda Saṃhitā. It is dug out with a spade, hence the name Abhrighātā. It is an antidote.

Amūlā–Some scholars call it Amarbela Bālihārī. It is used to poison arrows.

Arundhatī–Firstly, it fixes the acne, attaches the broken limbs etc. and is the lacquer emanating from Palāśa. Second, Arundhatī is a rich mammal, detoxifier, dehumidifier and chemist. It is the Sahadevī. The synonyms of Arundhati are Jībalā, Jīvantī, Sahamānā, Sahasvatī etc.

Arusrāṇa –It is called Āsrāvabheṣaja for diseases like bleeding, diarrhoea, urinary incontinence etc. The word Aru means acne and srāṇa means ripe.

Alasālā–This is safflower. Kauśika Sūtra calls it as the medicine of Ālaroga.

Āghāṭa – It is mentioned with the Arjuna tree and is a sign of Apāmārga.

Āṇḍika–It is an aquatic plant derived from an egg-shaped tuber. This is Kumuda.

Ābayu –It means mustard. Its seed oil and leafy vegetables are used medicinally in eye diseases etc.

Āsurī–It is the medicine for white leprosy. In the Bhāvaprakāśa Nighanṭu, Rāi is a synonym of Āsurī.

Āsrāvabheṣaja – It is a synonym for Darbha or Kuśa. Āsrāvabheṣaja herbs are used in wounds, bruises etc.

Iṭa – It is a monsoon sapling. Its decoction is used in fractures etc.

Iṣīkā –It is a kind of vegetation.

Kalmali–It removes faeces.

Kāndāviṣa- This is the poisonous tuber found in the mountains. It is applied to nullify the effects of poisoning. In the fourth maṇḍala of the Atharvaveda, it has been called an antidote drug.

Kilāsanāśana, Kilāsabheṣaja- These are anti-white leprosy drugs.

Kumuda- It is found in water and has lotus-like white flowers.

Kuṣṭha- This herb is in the Himālayas. Its phases are Nadyamāra, Nadyāriṣa and Nadyāya. It is antipyretic and defensive. It is applied to treat rheumatism, tuberculosis, short breath, eye diseases, headaches and dysentery.

Kūdī - This is the medicine of subjugation. This is Badarī.

Keśaddahanī, Keśavardhanī- It lengthens the hair and strengthens the hair follicles. It is Nitatnī medicine.

Kṣiptabheṣajī - In the Atharvaveda, pippalī tree is useful in the treatment of insanity. In the Bhāvaprakāśa, kṣiptabheṣajī is an appetite suppressant, semen enhancer and reduces fever, cough, etc.

Kṣetriyanāśanī - This herb is used to cure hereditary diseases and also heartburn, rakṣoghna, hair growth and śīrṣaroga.

Cīpudru- This is the pine tree. It is used as a medicine for Vidradhi, balāsa, blood bile, herpes and heart disease.

Jaṅgiḍa- It is called Viśvabheṣaja for longevity, kṛtyānāśaka and rakṣoghna.

Jīvantī - According to Sāyaṇa, this tree is always alive so it is called Jīvantī. It is a Pāṭhā or Pāṭā medicine for hair growth.

Jībalā- Jībalā means that which gives strength to live.

Talāśā, Talāśa - It is worn as a wristband. It is also called Tālīśa tree or Tālīśapatra. This herb is used in anorexia, dysentery, indigestion, and tuberculosis. It is always alive.

Tṛṣṭā, Tṛṣṭikā- Its synonyms are Viṣā, Viṣātakī, Ghuṇajambhanī, Āghujambhanī. Sāyaṇa calls it Bānāparṇī and Dāḍila Śarapuṅkhā. It is used to kill rats and insects. It is toxic and produces nausea and increases thirst.

Tṛṣṭāgha - It is applied as Mustard. Samidhā soaked in mustard oil is helpful for deworming.

Taudī- It is used as an antidote.

Tauvilikā, Trāyamāṇā – Its synonyms are Sahadevī, Sahamānā, Sahasvatī etc.

Daśabṛkṣa - Sāyaṇa has accepted ten trees as Daśabṛkṣa, namely- Palāśa, Udumvara etc. The maṇi made from the pieces of these trees is called the Daśabṛkṣa-maṇi beneficial for Arthritis.

Dipsauṣadhi –It is probably mothā or nāgaramothā. It is rakṣonāśaka and kṛtyānāśaka.

Nyastikā – This is a synonym of Subhaṅgakaraṇī and Sahasraparṇī. Sāyaṇa calls it Śaṅkhapuṣpī. This is good luck.

Puruṣavāra –It is applied as an antidote of snake-venom.

Puruṣāhṇa - Dāḍila calls it as the Palāśa tree which is applied in enemy destruction.

Palāśa- This is the sacrificial tree. Perimeter and yūpa are made with this tree in the Yajña. In Kauśika Sūtra, it is called Buddhi-vardhaka. Keśava calls it as the Sarvarogabhaiṣajjyaṃ. It is applied to relieve worms.

Piṅga- Piṅga is black mustard and Baja is white mustard. It prevents miscarriage.

Pippalī - This is Pīpara. Its synonyms are - Rasāyana, Kṣiptabheṣajī, Atividdhabheṣajī and Vātīkṛtabheṣajī. It is fed to all to cure and prevent rheumatism. This is also a talent enhancer. In the Bhāvaprakāśa Nighanṭu, it is said to be a destroyer of breath, cough, fever and dysentery.

Punarṇavā, Punarṇavā- This tree dries up in the summer and turns green again in the rainy season, so it is called Punarṇavā. These trees are of two types, white and red. Punarṇavā is the killer of Pāṇḍuroga, inflammation, poison, phlegm and stomach ailments. When bitten by poisonous insects and scorpions, its coating acts as a Mahauṣadhi.

Puṣpā - It is mentioned along with the Arundhatī tree. In the Keśava-paddhati, it is said that there is a provision to anoint this tree with Bhṛṅgarāja etc.

Pūtadru –Other names for this tree are Pūtudāru, Pītadāru, Devadāru, Pūtudru etc.

Paidva - This is a viṣoghna medicine and prevents snake venom and subdues snakes.

Pratisara - The importance of this maṇi has been described in twenty-two mantras of Atharva Veda. It eliminates diseases etc. This is the Tilaka tree. It helps to destroy enemies, rakṣonāśaka and kṛtyānāśaka.

Babhrū - It is an adjective of Sahasraparṇī or Śaṅkhapuṣpī. There are three types of Śaṅkhapuṣpī - white flower, blood flower and blue flower. It is beneficial for strength, agni-vardhaka, mental illness, worms and poisons.

Madāvatī - It is an alcoholic medicine and is probably Drākṣā or grapes.

Madugha, Madhūlaka - These two medicines are sweet. Their other names are-Maduga, Madhudugha, Jyeṣṭhī, Madhūka, Jeṭhīmadha, Mūlahaṭhī etc. It is an expectorant herb and laxative. This helps with a sore throat, urinary tract infections, colds and coughs.

Madhūka, Madhū –These two medicines are used for detoxification.

Madhujātā-Its synonyms are Madhū and Madhūka. This is applied to destroy snake venom.

Maṇḍūkī - This is Maṇḍūkaparṇī medicine. This is a variation of Brāhmī medicine. It is used as a medicine for urinary and genital herpes. It is also used in skin diseases, itching, leprosy, goitre etc.

Māṣaparṇī, Pṛśniparṇī- This medicine is used to prevent miscarriage. Keśava in the Kauśika Sūtra called it a very valuable medicine for women suffering from miscarriage. It is said to be used as a dermatological, rakṣonāśaka, rakṣośodhaka and nourishing medicine.

Mulālī - Sāyaṇa calls it Mṛnālī. This is a separate aquatic medicine.

Yakṣmānāśanī - It is a destroyer of waterborne diseases.

Rajanī-White leprosy and diarrhea are treated by Rajanī. Dāḍila, in the Kauśika Sūtra, calls it Methī. But, Keśava calls it as haridra.

Rāmā- Sāyaṇa understands it as Bhṛṅgarāja. It is applied with other medicines for white leprosy and white hair.

Ropaṇākā- According to Sāyaṇa, it is woodpecker or Sārikā bird. It is used to treat Kāmilā disease.

Rohaṇī - Sāyaṇa understands it to be lākṣā to repair broken bones and to fill old ones.

Lākṣā- Its other names are Arundhatī, Silācī, Sparaṇī, Niskṛti. It is used to repair broken bones and to fill old wounds.

Vaṭa - Banyan tree is also called as Nyagrodha, Raktaphala, Skandhaja, Kṣīrī, and Bahupāda etc. It is used to cure cough, bile, bronchitis, herpes, inflammation etc.

Varaṇa- Other names for it are Varuṇa, Setu, Tiktaśāka, Kumāraka. Varuṇa bark helps in digestion, acts as a tonic and is a laxative. It is also used as a medicine for Aśmarī disease, Mūtra-doṣa, Mūtrakṛccha, smallpox and tuberculosis.

Vātīkṛtabheṣajī - It is classified as Pippalī and is used in the treatment of rheumatism and insanity. If one eats Pippalī with molasses, then it cures indigestion, loss of appetite, respiratory diseases, heart disease, pāṇḍuroga and worms’ disease.

Vyāghramaṇi,Vyāghrī- This maṇi is made from tiger bones and claws. It is rakṣonāśaka and anthelmintic.

Śaṅkhapuṣpī, Nyastikā - The synonyms of these are Subhaṅgakaraṇī, Sahasraparṇī, Saṃvananī, Samuṣpalā, Babhrū etc.

Śatakāṇḍa- It is a word belonging to the phase of Darbha. Its synonyms are-Duścyavana, Sahasraparṇa, Ugrauṣadhi, Acchinnaparṇa, Sahasrakāṇḍa, Sahamāna etc. It has millions of Parva. It has been acknowledged as a wristband and life-enhancing.

Śatavāra - Śatavāramaṇi has been acknowledged as having many qualities. Sāyaṇa has interpreted the word Śatavāra as having hundred roots or branches and which destroys millions of diseases. It is used in piles, leprosy and itching.

Śaphaka - It is a plant similar to Kumuda etc. and is grown in waterbodies. Its leaves are shaped like a shaft; hence it is called Śaphaka. According to Āpastamba ŚrautaSūtra, it has been called an edible aquatic medicine.

Śilācī, Silācī – It is classified as Lākṣā. Its other names are- Jayantī, Sparaṇī, Arundhatī, Lākṣā, Niṣkṛti. This medicine is used to fix broken bones, wounds etc.

Śepaharṣaṇī- Its synonyms are Ucchuṣmā, Tanūvaśī. It is a medicine of Vājīkaraṇa. There is a story that this herb came out with Soma during the churning of the sea. It is very vigorous. He is called Soma’s brother.

Sadaṃpuṣpā, Sadaṃpuṣpī- It is called Sahasrākṣa and Sahasracakṣu. Dāḍila understands it as Trisandhyā and Keśava as Sandhyā. According to Sāyaṇa, its flowers are shaped like eyes. From this, divya śakti is gained. According to Kauśika Sūtra, it has been called a medicine of destroying snake venom and phlegm, headaches, toxins and bile. Its flowers bloom in every season.

Sāla, Śāla- According to Sāyaṇa, it is a tree. Pātañjala Mahābhāṣya calls it as Śālasāra and Khadirasāra. This tree is used as a medicine for cough, worms, ulcer, vaginal disease, corneal disease, gonorrhoea, poison and acne.

Hlādikā - This herb is in aquatic places. It is for comfort, so it is called Hlādikā. For Sāyaṇa, it is the Hlādaka medicine.

## Conclusion
Though the Atharvaveda properly recommends the use of various plants in the treatment of different diseases, yet from the high praise bestowed upon amulets or Maṇis, we can infer that these plants were mostly used as amulets.

Abhībartamaṇi increases state power and destroys the enemy. Indra was also conquered and prospered by Abhībartamaṇi. According to the Kauśika Sūtras, the method of making this maṇi is to keep gold in the middle and wrap it with iron, lead, silver and copper, and keep it in a pot of full of honey and yoghurt. After that, this maṇi is tied to the thread, placed on the kuśa and so on.

Parṇa-maṇi was prepared by the Soma-latā leaves or juice. Sāyaṇa says that Palāśa or Ḍhāka tree originated from the leaves of Soma. The force should bind and enchant the gem of the Palāśa tree to confirm strength, Āyu and wealth.

Ātharvaṇ medical literature is characterized by the use of water in various forms, an idea reinforced by the hymns. Primarily, all Ātharvaṇ rituals are to be performed with the assistance of single ritual fire and a single priest. The Ātharvaṇ medical practices are quasi-religious in which fire is ignited and libations are offered. Even then a distinction is made as ‘Pauṣṭika-vidhi’ (beneficial rites) and ‘Ghora-vidhi’ (witchcraft rites) to chase away the demons of diseases.
