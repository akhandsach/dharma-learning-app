Gurutva (Samskrit: गुरुत्वम्) is the twelfth guna or quality in the Praśastapāda-augmented Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika list of Padarthas. It has been rendered as heaviness, weight or gravity. It has a significant connotation in Physics.

## परिचयः ॥ Introduction
Gurutva, a guna (quality), specifically the 12th of the 24 qualities enumerated in the Navya-Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika texts, has been rendered as heaviness, weight or gravity in English. It has been mentioned in Maharshi Kaṇāda's 17 foundational Vaiseshika Sutras dealing with Gunas or qualities and from there has received attention through Praśastapāda's quality-list expansion to 24, followed by Śaṅkara Miśra's *Upaskāra* commentary (15th century), and the two great 17th-century Navya-Nyāya primers — Annaṃbhaṭṭa's *Tarka-saṃgraha* and Viśvanātha's *Bhāṣā-Pariccheda*. Jaina views from Kundakunda's *Pañcāstikāyasāra* (c. 2nd century CE), presents a dissenting theory which alone among classical Indian schools treated heaviness as a directly perceptible touch-sensation rather than as an inferred quality.

## The Position of Gurutva
The earliest stratum of the doctrine is found in the *Vaiśeṣika Sūtra* of Maharṣi Kaṇāda. His original *Vaiśeṣika Sūtras* enumerates only **17 qualities** (*guṇas*). The standard list given in Sūtra 1.1.6 — and repeated in various forms across texts — is:
 *rūpa* (colour), *rasa* (taste), *gandha* (smell), *sparśa* (touch), *saṅkhyā* (number), *parimāṇa* (dimension/measure), *pṛthaktva* (individuality), *saṃyoga* (conjunction), *vibhāga* (disjunction), *paratva* (priority), *aparatva* (posteriority), *buddhi* (cognition), *sukha* (pleasure), *duḥkha* (pain), *icchā* (desire), *dveṣa* (aversion), *prayatna* (effort)
Gurutva does *not* appear in this original list. It was added by **Praśastapāda** (c. 6th century CE) in his *Padārthadharma-saṃgraha* (also called *Praśastapāda-bhāṣya*), where seven additional qualities were mentioned bringing the total to 24:
* ***gurutva* (heaviness**)
* *dravatva* (fluidity)
* *sneha* (viscosity)
* *dharma* (merit)
* *adharma* (demerit)
* *śabda* (sound)
* *saṃskāra* (faculty/impressed energy)
The 24-guṇas oft quoted is the **Praśastapāda-augmented Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika** list, and not the original Maharshi Kaṇāda list. Gurutva is the *12th* quality in this augmented scheme. However, the fact that Maharshi Kaṇāda himself uses the term *gurutva* in sūtras 5.1.7, 5.1.18, and 5.2.3 — even though he doesn't list it among the *guṇas* in 1.1.6 — suggests that Praśastapāda's addition was a formalisation of what was already operative in Kaṇāda's reasoning.

Annaṃbhaṭṭa lists all 24 qualities in a single compound Sanskrit shloka:<blockquote>
रूपरसगन्धस्पर्शसङ्ख्यापरिमाणपृथक्त्वसंयोगविभागपरत्वापरत्वगुरुत्वद्रवत्वस्नेहशब्दबुद्धिसुखदुःखेच्छाद्वेषप्रयत्नधर्माधर्मसंस्काराश्चतुर्विंशतिगुणाः ॥ (4)

*rūpa-rasa-gandha-sparśa-saṅkhyā-parimāṇa-pṛthaktva-saṃyoga-vibhāga-paratva-aparatva-gurutva-dravatva-sneha-śabda-buddhi-sukha-duḥkha-icchā-dveṣa-prayatna-dharma-adharma-saṃskārāḥ caturviṃśati-guṇāḥ ॥ (4)*
</blockquote>In Annaṃbhaṭṭa's order, gurutva is the **twelfth** quality — appearing right after paratva (priority) and aparatva (posteriority), and immediately before dravatva (fluidity) and sneha (moisture).

= Vaiśeṣika Sūtras on Gurutva (Heaviness/Gravity) =
In the Vaiseshika Sutras we find the quality Gurutva in the context of falling objects.

### Sūtra 5.1.7 - Gurutva - cause of fall of object in absence of conjunction
संयोगाभावे गुरुत्वात् पतनम् ॥ ५.१.७ ॥ *saṃyogābhāve gurutvāt patanam* || 5.1.7 |

**Word-by-word:**
* *saṃyoga-abhāve* — in the absence of conjunction
* *gurutvāt* — from gravity / because of gurutva
* *patanam* — falling
**Translation (Sinha):** "In the absence of conjunction, falling (results) from gravity."

**According to the Upaskāra, the commentary of Śaṅkara Miśra,**"By the term 'conjunction,' every kind of impediment is indicated. Hence in the absence of impediments, in consequence of gravity as its non-combinative cause, falling — *i.e.*, an action resulting in conjunction below — is produced. Here in fruit, etc., possessing gravity, the impediment is conjunction; in a bird, etc., however, volition to hold up is the impediment to falling; in an arrow, etc., when discharged, it is the reaction that is the impediment to falling. The meaning is that, in the absence of these, falling is caused by gravity."

This is the foundational statement: gurutva is the cause of an object's falling **whenever** conjunction (i.e., any kind of restraint or support) is absent. A fruit on a branch does not fall — because it is conjoined to the branch; when the conjunction breaks, gurutva acts and the fruit falls.

### Sūtra 5.1.17 - Impetus in Projectile Motion
<blockquote>
नोदनादाद्यमिषोः कर्म तत्कर्मकारिताच्च संस्कारादुत्तरं तथोत्तरमुत्तरं च ॥ ५.१.१७ ॥ *nodanād ādyam iṣoḥ karma tat-karma-kāritāc ca saṃskārād uttaraṃ tathottaram uttaraṃ ca* || 5.1.17 ||
</blockquote>**Word-by-word:**
* *nodanāt* — from impulse / molecular movement
* *ādyam* — original, first
* *iṣoḥ* — of the arrow
* *karma* — action (motion)
* *tat-karma-kāritāt* — produced by that action
* *saṃskārāt* — from resultant energy (impressed momentum)
* *uttaraṃ tathottaram uttaraṃ ca* — the next, and the next, and the next
**Translation:** "The first action of the arrow is from impulse (*nodana*); the next is produced by that action's resultant energy (*saṃskāra*); and so on, the next, and the next."

This sūtra is significant in the history of physics because it gives a remarkably early account of what we now call **impetus** or **inertia**. The arrow continues to move not by repeated pushes but because each instant of motion produces a *saṃskāra* (a persisting impressed energy) that generates the next instant of motion. S.D. Chatterjee notes that this Vaiśeṣika *vega-saṃskāra* concept "closely fits in with the modern concept of momentum" and is "the nearest approach to Newton's first law of motion" in classical Indian thought.

### Sūtra 5.1.18 - Gurutva - cause of eventual fall of object in projectile motion
<blockquote>
संस्काराभावे गुरुत्वात् पतनम् ॥ ५.१.१८ ॥ *saṃskārābhāve gurutvāt patanam* || 5.1.18 ||
</blockquote>**Word-by-word:**
* *saṃskāra-abhāve* — in the absence of saṃskāra (impressed energy)
* *gurutvāt* — from gravity
* *patanam* — falling
**Translation:** "In the absence of saṃskāra (impressed energy), falling (results) from gravity."

While the arrow's *saṃskāra* lasts, the arrow continues forward; but *saṃskāra* gradually exhausts itself (by doing work against air, gravity, and other counteracting forces), and when it is gone, gurutva once again takes over and the arrow falls to the earth.

### Significance
Sutra (5.1.18) completes the projectile-motion imagery. On observing the parallel construction with 5.1.7 — the same idea
 absence (of conjunction/of samskara) + gurutva + patanam
signals that gurutva is the *standing cause* of falling, neutralised only by conjunction (5.1.7) or by impressed energy (5.1.18).

### Sūtra 5.2.3 - Gurutva extended to liquids
<blockquote>
अपां संयोगाभावे गुरुत्वात् पतनम् ॥ ५.२.३ ॥ *apāṃ saṃyogābhāve gurutvāt patanam* || 5.2.3 ||
</blockquote>**Word-by-word:**
* *apām* — of waters (genitive plural of *ap*)
* *saṃyoga-abhāve* — in the absence of conjunction
* *gurutvāt* — from gravity
* *patanam* — falling
**Translation:** "Of waters, in the absence of conjunction, falling (results) from gravity."

This is the sūtra that **explicitly extends gurutva to liquids** (*ap* / *jala* — the water element of the Pañchamahābhūtas). The same concept *saṃyogābhāve gurutvāt patanam* from 5.1.7 is repeated, but now prefixed with *apām* — "of waters." Rainwater falls from the sky for exactly the same reason that the fruit falls from the branch: once the conjunction (with the cloud, in the case of rain) is removed, gurutva acts.

This sūtra is the textual basis for the doctrine that **gurutva resides in the atoms of both pṛthvī (earth) and ap (water)**, and in no other element. Praśastapāda's *Padārthadharma-saṃgraha* and Śrīdhara's *Nyāyakandalī* both record this: gurutva is "an imperceptible quality of the *dravyas* pṛthvī and *jala*." Fire, air, and ākāśa do not possess gurutva; if anything, they tend to ascend or remain steady.

= गुरुत्व-सिद्धान्ताः ॥ Gurutva Doctrines =

### प्रशस्तपादभाष्ये गुरुत्व-सिद्धान्तः॥ Praśastapāda's Gurutva Siddhanta
**Praśastapāda** (*Padārthadharma-saṃgraha*) gives the definitive elaboration of gurutva in his bhāṣya. Three points become canonical from this text onwards:
# **Gurutva is the non-inherent cause of falling.** Praśastapāda defines gurutva as "the *asamavāyi-kāraṇa* (non-inherent cause) of the first act of falling."
# **Gurutva is invisible (atīndriya).** It cannot be perceived by any sense organ. Its existence is inferred only from its effect — the act of falling itself.
# **Gurutva resides in earth and water atoms (paramāṇus).** It is eternal (*nitya*) in the atoms themselves; non-eternal (*anitya*) in composite bodies built from those atoms.

### अन्नमभट्टस्य गुरुत्व-लक्षणम् ॥ Annaṃbhaṭṭa's Gurutva Lakshana
Annaṃbhaṭṭa's *Tarka-saṃgraha*, composed in the second half of the 17th century CEis the standard introductory primer of the joint Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika school. Its central project is to give clear lakṣaṇas (definitions) for the seven padārthas and their subdivisions, including the 24 qualities.

Tarka-samgraha gives a lucid and clear definition of gurutva as "the non-inherent cause of the first act of falling" (*patanādya-asamavāyi-kāraṇam*). The word *ādya* ("first") is important: it distinguishes gurutva from *vega* (impetus), which is the non-inherent cause of *subsequent* falling. So gurutva initiates the fall; vega sustains it.<blockquote>
आद्यपतनासमवायिकारणं गुरुत्वम् । पृथिवीजलवृत्ति ॥ २९ ॥

ādya-patana-asamavāyi-kāraṇaṃ gurutvam | pṛthivī-jala-vṛtti ॥ 29
</blockquote>**Word-by-word**
* *ādya* — first, initial
* *patana* — falling, the act of falling
* *asamavāyi-kāraṇam* — non-inherent cause / non-material cause
* *gurutvam* — heaviness, gurutva
* *pṛthivī-jala-vṛtti* — residing in earth and water (literally: having pṛthivī and jala as its locus)
**Translation**: *"Weight is the non-material cause of the first moment of falling of an object. It exists in Earth and Water."* V. N. Jha further defines gurutva as "weight (gravitation)" — "A solid object falls down because of weight."

### = Four substantive aspects=
**Aspect 1 — Gurutva is a cause (kāraṇa), not a force or quantity.** Annaṃbhaṭṭa places gurutva within the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika causation framework, which recognizes three kinds of cause:
* *samavāyi* (inherent) - material cause
* *asamavāyi* (non-inherent) - non-material cause
* *nimitta* (efficient) - instrumental cause
**Aspect 2 — Gurutva is the non-inherent cause.** The samavāyi-kāraṇa of falling is the body itself; the asamavāyi-kāraṇa is the gurutva-quality *in* the body; the nimitta-kāraṇa is the absence of conjunction or impetus. Jha translates *asamavāyi-kāraṇa* as "non-material cause."

**Aspect 3 — Gurutva causes the FIRST fall, not the subsequent fall.** *ādya* ("first, initial") is perhaps the most ingenious word in the entire definition. As the *Tarka-saṃgraha-dīpikā* itself spells out, gurutva is responsible only for the initial instant of falling. The continuing fall — at each subsequent instant — is caused by *vega* (impetus), which is itself the asamavāyi-kāraṇa of the subsequent fall. The Dīpikā says explicitly: *"The word ādya is added in this definition to avoid over-pervasion to vega (velocity), because vega is the asamavāyi-kāraṇa of the subsequent falling."*

**Aspect 4 — Gurutva resides in earth and water only.** The clause *pṛthivī-jala-vṛtti* formalises what Maharshi Kaṇāda established in Vaiśeṣika Sūtra 5.2.3. Gurutva does not reside in fire, air, ether, time, space, self, or mind.

### दीपिकाभाष्ये विवरणम् ॥ Elaborations from the Dīpikā
Annaṃbhaṭṭa's own commentary, the *Tarka-saṃgraha-dīpikā*, adds two important elaborations beyond the root-text:

**Elaboration 1 — Gurutva is imperceptible (atīndriya).** The Dīpikā emphasises that gurutva is invisible — it cannot be perceived by any of the five sense organs. It is known only by inference (*anumāna*), from its effect — the act of falling. We see the apple fall; from this perceived effect we infer the gurutva quality in the apple.

**Elaboration 2 — Eternal in atoms, non-eternal in composites.** Drawing on Śrīdhara's *Nyāyakandalī*, the Dīpikā affirms that gurutva is eternal in the *paramāṇus* (atoms) of earth and water, but non-eternal in composite bodies (*kārya-dravya*) made from those atoms. When a pot breaks, the pot's gurutva is destroyed along with it — but the gurutva of the individual earth-atoms that made up the pot persists unchanged.

**Śrīdhara** in the *Nyāyakandalī* makes the key ontological point: gurutva is **eternal in the paramāṇus** (atoms) of earth and water, but **non-eternal in composite bodies** (gross objects). When a clay pot is destroyed, its gurutva is destroyed with it — but the gurutva of the earth-atoms that made up the pot continues unchanged.

### भाषा-परिच्छेदे गुरुत्व-विवरणम् ॥ Gurutva in Bhāṣā-Pariccheda
Vishvanatha in his Bhasa-Parriccheda independently confirms the Tarka-Samgraha doctrine about Gurutva as follows<blockquote>
अतीन्द्रिय गुरुत्व स्यात पृथिव्यादि द्वये तु तत् । १५३ atīndriya gurutva syāta pṛthivyādi dvaye tu tat | 153
</blockquote>**Translation**: Weight is imperceptible to the senses. It abides in the two substances beginning with the earth.

S. C. Chatterjee's *The Nyāya Theory of Knowledge* (1939) draws on Viśvanātha's text to frame the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika view:<blockquote>
*"Almost all the Indian systems agree in holding that heaviness or weight (gurutva) is a quality which cannot be perceived, but must be inferred. The reason why these qualities are treated as imperceptible is that they cannot be perceived by any of the senses recognised in Indian philosophy. They are supersensible entities and hence there cannot be any kind of contact between these and our senses."*
</blockquote>

### Why gurutva must be inferred — the Navya-Nyāya argument
Based on the commentaries discussed above gurutva-as-inferred is essentially unanimous across the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika tradition. The chain of agreement runs:
* **Kaṇāda** via Śaṅkara Miśra's Upaskāra: "Though imperceptible, gravity can be inferred from the action of falling."
* **Praśastapāda** (6th c. CE): Gurutva is invisible, inferred only from its effect.
* **Annaṃbhaṭṭa** (17th c. CE), via the Tarka-saṃgraha-dīpikā: gurutva is *atīndriya*, known only by anumāna.
* **Viśvanātha** (17th c. CE), Bhāṣā-Pariccheda 153: same view.
The argument depends on a specific Nyāya doctrine of perception: every act of perception requires *sannikarṣa* (sense-object contact). Six kinds of contact are recognised in standard Navya-Nyāya. For gurutva to be perceived, the skin would have to be in *saṃyukta-samavāya* with the heavy object — sensing the object and then sensing the gurutva inhering in it.

The Naiyāyikas say this doesn't happen. What the skin senses when one lifts a heavy stone is *pressure* (a *saṃyoga*, conjunction) — not *gurutva* (a separate quality of the object). The pressure-sensation is a touch-experience that belongs to the perceiver. The gurutva is an objective quality that resides in the stone. To get from the experience to the quality, an inference is needed.

### = Disagreement by Jainas=
The Jainas dissented from the Nyaya view — and their dissent is recorded clearly in Kundakunda's *Pañcāstikāyasāra* (*Pañcāstikāya-sāra*, *Essence of the Five Existents*). They recognised only four elements (earth, water, fire, air) and not *ākāśa* as a material element and held that heaviness (*gauratva*) and lightness (*lāghava*) are simply **special kinds of touch**, perceptible directly through the skin. According to Jainas, when you pick up a heavy stone, the heaviness is *not* something you infer from the pressure; the heaviness *is* the perception.

The Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika system, by contrast, held that what you actually perceive when you lift the stone is a series of touch-sensations (pressure on the palm, strain in the muscles), from which you *infer* the imperceptible quality of gurutva that resides in the stone.

This disagreement is philosophically valid as it maps onto a genuine difference about what perception is — whether perception is direct acquaintance with qualities themselves, or whether perception is a sense-experience from which qualities are inferred. The Jainas took the former position; the Naiyāyikas and Vaiśeṣikas took the latter. Both positions have modern descendants in philosophy of perception.

It is also worth noting that the Jaina position partially aligns with the modern scientific view: when we step on a weighing scale, the gauge reading is in some sense a *direct measurement* of weight, not an inference from a separate observable. The Jainas, in admitting heaviness as a touch-sensation, were closer to this view than the Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika tradition was.

= संवादः ॥ Discussion =
It is sometimes claimed in popular literature that Kaṇāda's gurutva is "the same as Newton's gravity." The sūtras here show what it really is and is not.

**What Gurutva IS:**
# **A qualitative causal principle (a guṇa)** — specifically, the non-inherent cause of the first instant of falling.
# **The same principle for stones and for rainwater** — it operates in both earth and water atoms, unifying solid-fall and liquid-fall.
# **Neutralised by enumerable impediments** — conjunction (5.1.7), impressed energy (5.1.18), volitional effort, adṛṣṭa, mantra, magnetism — all enumerated by Śaṅkara Miśra in his commentary on 5.1.7.
# **Eternal in atoms, non-eternal in composites** — a sophisticated atomic-versus-aggregate distinction that anticipates the modern idea that the mass of a macroscopic object derives from the masses of its constituent particles.
# **Imperceptible but inferable** — a hidden quality known only through its observable effect, the act of falling itself.
**What it is NOT:**
# **A quantitative force producing acceleration proportional to mass.** Both *The Cultural Heritage of India* Vol. VI (Chatterjee, p. 104) and the *Concise History of Science in India* (Subbarayappa ed., p. 470) are explicit: "there seems to be no correlation between gravity and the mass of the substance." **Gurutva is not F = ma.**
# **A universal attractive force between bodies.** There is no mutual attraction between, say, the earth and the moon in the Vaiśeṣika scheme. Falling is simply the natural action of gurutva-possessing atoms once impediments are removed.
# **A correlate of mass or volume.** Gurutva (the 12th quality) and *parimāṇa* (the 6th quality — measure/dimension) are treated as two *independent* qualities in the Tarka-saṃgraha, with no derivational relation between them. The Vaiśeṣikas do not say *"a body with larger parimāṇa has greater gurutva"*. The bridge between weight and volume — via density — is not part of the classical Indian texts.
The deep insight is genuine: that there is *something* about earth and water atoms that makes them fall, and that this applies to a stone or a drop of rain. The conceptual bridge to Newton was simply not built. Gurutva is a precise classical-Indian *philosophical* doctrine about heaviness; Newtonian gravity is a precise modern *mathematical* doctrine about gravitation. They share a topic, not a theory.
