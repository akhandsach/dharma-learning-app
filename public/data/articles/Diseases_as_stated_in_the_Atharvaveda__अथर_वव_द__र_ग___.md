The hymns of the Atharvaveda mention nearly a hundred diseases—both, major and minor as well as known and unknown. The term used for disease throughout the text is “yakṣma’ though the word “roga’ also appears occasionally. According to the earliest view of disease held by the ancient Indians, all morbid and abnormal states of the body and mind for which no special reason is assignable, are brought on by attacks of demons. Thus, in the medical charms of the Atharvaveda the diseases are constantly addressed as demoniacal beings. Disease is seen as the manifestation of the will of a supernatural power. To the Atharvan, this power was generally one of the hosts of demons by which he believed himself to be surrounded. Thus, yakṣma is actually such a malevolent force that is characterised as entering and possessing each and every part of the body.

## Fever ‘*Takman*’
Fever has been described as '<nowiki/>*Takman*<nowiki/>' in the Atharvaveda. '<nowiki/>*Takman'<nowiki/>* could only be found in the Atharvaveda and is mentioned nowhere else. '<nowiki/>*Takman*' is created when fire penetrates water. This means that the reason behind fever is when fire and thunder penetrate water causing rain. This fever is the flame of fire—it is like a furnace or fire borne out of the log. The Atharvaveda discusses the disease *Takman* with great seriousness. This is inferred from the considerable number of hymns that speak of it – I.25; V.4; V.22; VI.20, VII.116; XIX.39 as well as from a plethora of references to it in the text – IV.9.8; V.4.1,9; V.30.16; VI.20.1,2,4; IX.8.3; XI.2.22,26; XII.5.39.

This disease has been dealt with in Atharvaveda with great seriousness. Takman is personified in a charm addressing it as a superior wrathful force that needs to be placated with supplication and prayer. Takman is treated by addressing Agni. Some of the types of Takman are suggested to be treated also by herbal remedy. It is recorded that the progress of fever is accompanied by conditions like cold, hot, cough etc. There are different charms prescribed as associated with Takman. Veneration is rendered to Rudra, Varuṇa, heaven, earth, plants and various forms of Takman. Kuṣṭha is regarded as the most formidable among all plants. This flower is found in the mountains and restores health. This plant is known by three names namely, Nadya, Nadyamāra and Nadyāriṣa. Fever, headache and cough are believed to be caused by powerful lightning. In the charm against cough, the cough is asked to fly away, like the arrow, the rays of the sun and like the soul following its desire. In the charm against Balāsa, pain is offered. In the case of Takman, the patient is given a gruel made of roasted grain in a copper vessel. This disease attacks repeatedly and periodically. When Takman occurs throughout the year, this is called Hāyana. In this way, different names are given for the fever occuring in different seasons. This disease resembles malaria. It is said that the slaves, menials and low people suffered from Takman. Another variety of Takman, bilious Typhoid is also found in the Atharvaveda. In this way, Atharvaveda records various types of fever. Takman is directly called the son of Varuṇa. It is referred in the Kauśika Sūtra that Takman is caused by inimical witchcraft. Takman is prevalent in some parts of the country like Mūjavat, Mahāvṛṣa, Bālhīka, Gandhāra, the Aṅga, and the Magadha. So Takman was both caused by witchcraft and through the infected locality. Añjana is very much relieving to the burning body suffering from Takman. According to the Vedic text, Takman has various names such as Arciḥ, Tapuḥ, Śociḥ, Śokaḥ, Abhiśokaḥ, Varuṇasya putraḥ, Hrūduḥ etc. Fever is imagined having three legs and a large abdomen, with ashes on the body, wearing a tiger skin, with a pale complexion, bright body, brown eyes and short thighs. Fever causes both physical and psychological ailment. Physical defects include Arthritis, bile and phlegm and psychological problems include Rājasa and Tāmasa. The different treatments of fever include Yajña, water treatment, air and sun treatment. Holding the maṇi also relieves fever. According to Atharvaveda, Gugala or Guggulu is a fever destroyer.

## Some specific Charms
A.V. I. 2 is a charm against fever (jvara), diarrhoea (atīsāra), diabetes (atimūtra) and glandular sores (nāḍī-maṇa). A string made of muñja grass is to be tied, the mud from a field or ant-hill is to be drunk, clarified butter is to be applied and the holes of the anus and penis and the openings of the sores are to be aerated with a leather bladder and the charm is to be chanted. The disease āsrāva mentioned in this hymn, is explained by Sāyaṇa as meaning diabetes (mūtrātisāra).

AV I. 3 is a charm against stoppage of urine and stool (mūtra-purīṣa-nirodha). Along with a chanting of the hymn the patient is to be made to drink either earth from a rat’s hole (mūṣika-mṛttikā), a pūtikā plant, curd, or saw-dust from old wood, or he is to ride an elephant or a horse, or to throw an arrow; a fine iron needle was to be passed through the urinal canal. This is probably the earliest stage of what developed in later times as the vasti-kriyā.

AV I.7 and 1.8 are charms for driving away evil spirits, yātudhānas and kimīdins, when a man is possessed by them.

AV I.10 is a charm for dropsy (jalodara): a jugful of water containing grass, etc. is to be sprinkled over the body of the patient.

AV I.11 is a charm for securing easy delivery.

AV I.12 is a charm for all diseases arising from disturbance of vāta, pitta and śleṣman—fat, honey and clarified butter or oil have to be drunk. Head-disease (śīrṣakti) and cough (kāsa) are specially mentioned.

AV I.17 is a charm for stopping blood from an injury of the veins or arteries or for stopping too much haemorrhage of women. In the case of such injuries, a handful of street-dust is to be thrown on the place of injury or a bandage is to be tied with sticky mud.

AV I.22 is a charm against jaundice and heart diseases. This is addressed to the patient wherein some remedial procedures are mentioned. First, it is said that patients suffering (heartache) and jaundice shall leave him and go up to the sun. The patient is then informed that he is being enveloped in the colour of the red bull, in red hues unto long life. Prayers are offered for the patient’s recovery and complete relief from yellow colour–the colour of jaundice. It is then said that the patient is being enveloped in every form and strength of Rohiṇī–the divinity of the cows. The colour red (Rohiṇī) becomes important. The jaundice is transferred through chants into birds like parrots, thrushes and yellow wagtail. 

AV I.23 is a charm against kilāsa or kuṣṭha (white leprosy) of the bone, flesh and skin and the disease by which hairs are turned grey (palita). The white parts are to be rubbed with an ointment made of cow-dung, bhṛṅga-rāja, haridrā indravanmī and nīlikā until they appear red. The black medicines applied are asked to turn the white parts black.

AV I.25 is a charm against takman or fever—the patient has to be sprinkled with the water in which a red-hot iron axe has been immersed. The description shows that it was of the malarial type; it came with cold (śīta) and a burning sensation (śoci). Three types of this fever are described: that which came the next day (anyedyuḥ), the second day (ubhayedyuḥ), or the third day (tṛtīyaka). It was also associated with yellow, probably because it produced jaundice.

AV II.7.1 is a charm for removing all hereditary (kṣetrīya) diseases; the horn of a deer is to be used as an amulet.<blockquote>
अघद्विष्टा देवजाता वीरुच्छपथयोपनी। आपो मलमिव प्राणैक्षीत्सर्वान्मच्छपथाँ अधि ॥ 1॥ aghadviṣṭā devajātā vīrucchapathayopanī| āpo malamiva prāṇaikṣītsarvānmacchapathām adhi || 1||
</blockquote>Meaning: Just as the diseases of the body are cured by good medicine and dirty clothes etc. are purified by water, similarly even sinful and evil-doing people get freed from sins by the knowledge of Brahma and become pure souls and by thinking about the blessings of God, they become charitable and enjoy eternal happiness.

AV II.9 and 10 are charms against hereditary (kṣetrīya) diseases, leprosy, dyspepsia, etc. Amulets of arjuna wood, barley, sesamum and its flowers had also to be tied when the charm was uttered.

AV II.31 is a charm against various diseases due to worms. The priest, when uttering this charm, should hold street-dust in his left hand and press it with his right hand and throw it on the patient. There are visible and invisible worms. They are generated in the intestines, head and heels. They go about through the body by diverse ways and cannot be killed even with various kinds of herbs. They sometimes reside in the hills and forests and in herbs and animals, and they enter into our system through sores in the body and through various kinds of food and drink.

AV II.33 is a charm for removing yakṣman from all parts of the body.

AV IV.4 is a charm for attaining virility— the roots of the kapittha tree boiled in milk are to be drunk when the charm is uttered.

AV IV.6 and 7 are charms against vegetable poisoning—the essence of the kṛmuka tree is to be drunk.

AV V.4 is a charm against fever (takman) and phthisis; the patient is to take the herb kuṣṭha with butter when the charm is uttered.

AV V.11 is a charm against fever.

AV V.23 is a charm against worms—the patient is given the juice of the twenty kinds of roots.

AV VI.15 is a charm for eye-diseases; the patient has to take various kinds of vegetable leaves fried in oil, particularly the mustard plant.

AV VI.20 is a charm against Takman or fever. It is hoped that Takman would pass like a blithering drunkard. Takman is asked to assail the other and spare the Vedic self. It is also a charm against bilious fever (śuṣmiṇo jvarasya); which produces great burning sensation, delirium and jaundice.

AV VI.21 is a charm for increasing the hair—the hair is to be sprinkled with a decoction of various herbs.

AV VI.23 is a charm against heart-disease, dropsy and jaundice.

AV VI.25 is a charm for inflammation of the glands of the neck (gaṇḍa-māla).

AV VI.85 is a charm against consumption (rāja-yakṣman).

AV VI.105 is a charm against cough. The cough is asked to fly away and three analogies are used in this regard. The cough is requested to imitate the flight of the arrow, the rays of the sun and fly like the soul following its desire.

AV VI.109 is a charm for rheumatic diseases (vāta-vyādhi).

AV VI.127 is a charm for abscess (vidradha), phlegmatic diseases (valāsa) and erysipelatous inflammation (visarpa).

## Surgery in Atharvaveda
In the seventh chapter of the Atharvaveda, specifically in the 78th hymn, four mantras make reference to a unique medical condition known as gandamala. This ailment is characterized by the presence of small, red lumps. The significance of gandamala lies in its association with the practice of surgical treatment as mentioned in the Atharvaveda. These mantras bear testimony to the profound understanding of surgical concepts possessed by the sages and seers of that era, illustrating their advanced approach to medicine and healing.

## Brain Disorders
In the Ninth Chapter of the Atharvaveda, one will get the remedies for Brain or Head Disorders. In the Atharvaveda’s ninth chapter, the 13th hymn, encompassing 22 verses, elaborates upon ailments affecting various parts of the human body, including the head, ears, nose, abdomen, anus, intestines, feet, knees, phlegm, and cough. These verses provide a comprehensive discourse on the eradication of such maladies through the recitation of sacred mantras. Moreover, the hymn highlights the curative potential of the rays of the rising sun, underscoring a profound scientific understanding of their therapeutic efficacy.

## Serpent Venom
In the tenth chapter of the Atharvaveda, in the fourth hymn consisting of 26 verses, Sage Garutman expresses his profound reflections on the neutralization of serpent venom. In a land as abundant with serpents as India, these creatures are not only perceived as formidable adversaries but are also revered as divine beings, an intrinsic aspect of Indian culture. This section of the scripture reveals the application of these hymns, along with numerous references to various species of serpents.

## Krimi etc.
Many references are available in Atharvaveda about Kṛmi (Bacteria). The word Kṛmi (nourished by raw flesh), Rākhasa (which sucks blood), Yatudhana (which causes pain), Piśāca (eats flesh), Apsarā (swims in water), Gāndharva (makes noise) etc. have been used to denote ‘micro-organisms in Atharvaveda’. Nearly 100 types of organisms are described—some of them are Dṛṣṭa (macroscopic) and the others are Adṛṣṭa (microscopic). Different types of organisms, the place where they dwell, their shape, types, the harm caused by them, signs and symptoms, line of treatment, use of fumigation, to control such organisms etc. are also described in Atharvaveda.

Atharvaveda does not consider Yakṣmā to have consumption, but it is a general disease. It decays the bone of the spine and affects flesh and bone marrow. The patient should be given porridge of rotten fish and bath with warm water containing burning sesame hemp, cow dung etc. The treatment of consumption is also done with charms and amulets.

Varuṇa is said to send dropsy to punish crime, especially falsehood (Atharvaveda I.10.1-4; II.10.1). Rudra’s arrows are believed to cause tumours (Atharvaveda VI.57); *takman* and *kāsikā* (cough) are his weapons (Atharvaveda X.2.22). Diarrhoea is connected with the arrows of Parjanya (Atharvaveda I.2), and lightning (Agni) with fever, headache and cough in (Atharvaveda I.12). Headache, ear and eye diseases as well as intestinal diseases are attributed to worms; worms in children and in cattle also find special mention in the hymns.

Heart-disease and phthisis are also mentioned in Atharvaveda. There are said to be a hundred kinds of death (mṛtyu) (AV VIII.5.7), which are explained by Sāyaṇa as meaning diseases such as fever, head-disease, etc. Several diseases are mentioned in IX.18—first the diseases of the head, śīrṣakti, śīrṣāmaya, karṇa-śūla and visalpaka, by which secretions of bad smell come out from the ear and the mouth, then fever proceeding from head troubles with shivering and cracking sensations in the limbs. Then comes consumption; followed by valāsa, kāhābāha of the abdomen, diseases of kloma, the abdomen, navel and heart, diseases of the spine, the ribs, the eyes, the intestines, the visalpa, vidradha , wind-diseases (vāṭīkāra), alaji and diseases of the leg, knee, pelvis, veins and head.

## Pittaja diseases
The types of well-known Pittaja diseases are—Atisāra, Āmātisāra, Viśarīka, Raktātisāra, Raktapitta and Raktavamana etc. In the *Bhāvaprakāśa*, Atisāra or diarrhea is compared to a flow in river of manure. This is a serious disease. Atisāra can be cured with Bilva. The well-known types of Vātaja roga are: arthritis or Vāta, respiratory diseases or Śvāsa, Joint arthritis or Sandhi-vāta and hip-gout or Kaṭi-vāta. *Bhāvaprakāśa* says that the following foods increase Vāta namely, Prasādhikā, Khesārī, Kalāi, gram, Śyāmā, Mug, Aḍahara, Rājamāṣa, Moṭha, Varāṭikā, Musurikā and Kodrava. The causes for Vāta disease are the breath in the eastern air, staying up during the night, onerous labour, starvation, erosion of bodily minerals, sorrow, anxiety etc. Atharvaveda says Pippalī is medicine for Vātaroga. It is a life-giving medicine. It cures Unmāda-roga, Vāta-roga and Pakṣaghāta.

## Respiratory Disorders
Atharvaveda discusses Śvāsa-roga i.e. Respiratory problems. Soma medicine has been spoken about in Ṛgveda, Yajurveda and Atharvaveda. Somalatā is called the Svāmī of medicines. Somalatā juice can be used to treat breathing problems, cough and hiccups. It is also useful for the following diseases–Jalodara, Śvāsaroga, Hṛdayaroga, Rohiṇī-roga etc. In Atharvaveda, Pippalī, Talāśā and Vacātree etc can be used to treat Śvāsaroga. Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā and Atharvaveda speak of Śamī tree.

## Joint Pain
Atharvaveda discusses Sandhi-Vāta-roga. Sandhi means joint in the body. If polluted air permeates the joints, then the joints lose their strength and start paining and become distended. This is called Sandhi-vāta. Atharvaveda talks about the treatment of Sandhi-vāta by Daśavṛkṣa or Daśamūla. Pūtudāru is also a cure for Sandhi-vāta. Atharvaveda also talks about Kaṭi-vāta or waist pain.

## Infectious Diseases etc
Atharvaveda talks about Chuta-roga or infectious disease. Atharvaveda says that dwelling with the bad people, eating food with them can also cause many types of infectious diseases. This means that for getting cured, one must renounce the company of the bad people and take Apāmārga medicine.

The Atharvaveda mentions Viśara diseases which cause pain and fractures of the parts. The Atharvaveda speaks of the application of Jaṅgiḍa maṇi for the treatment of this disease.

Viṣūcikā disease, called cholera, is mentioned in Yajurveda. The word Viṣūcikā for this disease is found in the Atharvaveda and the god Rudra is said to be the destroyer of this disease. The Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā speaks of the application of Upavāka or Upavākā medicine. The Atharvaveda mentions the application of Kuṣṭha medicine for this disease.  Rājayakṣmā is such a disease that takes away all the vitality of the body. According to Atharvaveda, there are different medicines to treat Balāsa like Cīpudru tree, Jaṅgiḍa or Arjuna, Añjana, Tejana, Vaṃśa. There are four symptoms of Royal tuberculosis like eating unwholesome diet, loss of sperm, etc. The symptoms include excessive heat on the shoulders and sights, pain in limbs and maximum fever.

## Conclusion
Apart from the ordinary diseases, many were the cases of possession by demons and evil spirits, of which we have quite a large number. Some of the prominent ones are: Yātudhāna, Kimīdin, Piśāca, Piśācī, Amīvā, Dvayāvin, Rakṣaḥ, Magundī, Alitnśa, Vatsapa, Palāla, Anupalāla, Śarku, Koka, Malimluca, Palijaka, Vavrivāsas, Āśreṣa, Rkṣagrīva, Pramīlin, Durṇāmā, Sunāmā, Kukṣila, Kusūla, Kakubha, Śrima, Arāya, Karuma, Khalaja, Śakadhūmaja, Uruṇḍa, Matmata, Kumbhamuṣka, Sāyaka, Nagnaka, Taṅgalva, Pavīnasa, Gandharva, Brahmagraha etc.

Some of the diseases with their troublesome symptoms were (poetically) personified, and diseases which often went together were described as being related as brothers and sisters. There were also the diseases due to sorcery, which played a very important part as an offensive measure in Vedic India. Many of the diseases were also known to be hereditary (kṣetrīya). From the names of the diseases mentioned above it will be found that most of the diseases noted by Charaka existed in the Vedic age.

The view-point from which the Vedic people looked at diseases seems to have always distinguished the different diseases from their symptoms. Thus, the fever was that which produced shivering, cold, burning sensation, and the like, i.e. the diagnosis was mainly symptomatic. In addition to the charms and amulets, and the herbs which were to be internally taken, water was considered to possess great medical and life-giving properties. There are many hymns which praise these qualities of water. The medicinal properties of herbs were often regarded as being due to water, which formed their essence. Scanty references to diseases and their cures are found sparsely scattered in other Rg-Vedic texts and Brāhmaṇas. But nothing in these appears to indicate any advance on the Atharvaveda in medical knowledge.
